Energy Efficiency in the Power grid

By: Anthony Leonard WRIT 105SW: Science Writing for the Public

In California, the power grid produced for the state 203 million megawatt hours in 2022. This was the fourth highest amount of energy produced in the United States. A single megawatt hour represents nearly 2 average California houses monthly electricity usage[#]. The sources for this energy are distributed between renewables, fossil fuels, and nuclear power. The 203 million MWhs is what is available on the power grid over the year, however, the power that was used to make that energy is significantly higher than how much can be used.

Each of the non renewable energy sources are inefficient with the energy used to operate the power plants. The most productive energy source currently is natural gas, also being the most efficient, but for the 96 million MWhs that it produced in 2022, the power plants released the equivalent of 54 million MWhs as heat and waste. For the 114 million MWhs produced through fossil fuels and nuclear energy, there is a total waste energy of 159 million MWhs.

In the images below I have marked the lost electricity as it goes through the power grid. The first white image is the total electricity equivalent that is put in to the power plants. As it leaves the power plants all of the BLUE colored area is lost to heat and waste. Already 58% of the energy is already gone. In TEAL is the 5% of the electricity that is lost in the power lines and power stations between the power plants and the end user. In GREEN is the amount of the energy that is lost in the use of battery storage, which loses 10% of the electricity that it interacts with. Currently this is on the lower end, but battery storage is one of the most important parts of the renewable based power grid, so it will grow with time.

This leaves the white area remaining at the end of the graph, accounting for 40% of the input energy for non-renewable energies, and representing 43% of the total usage of California in 2022.
